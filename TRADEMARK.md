# SOLECTRUS Trademark Policy

The **SOLECTRUS** name and the SOLECTRUS logo identify the SOLECTRUS project by
Georg Ledermann.

Georg Ledermann claims trademark rights in the SOLECTRUS name and logo based on
their continuous use in connection with the project. These trademark rights are
separate from the copyright license that applies to the SOLECTRUS source code.

This policy exists to protect users: when you see the SOLECTRUS name or logo, you
should be able to trust that the project, image, or service really comes from the
official source — and not from a modified copy.

## In short

**The code is open — the name is not.** Anyone may use, study, modify, and
redistribute the software under the AGPL. But nobody may present their own fork,
modified version, or paid/hosted service as "SOLECTRUS".

- **You may:** use and change the code; refer to SOLECTRUS truthfully
  ("compatible with SOLECTRUS"); redistribute the **unmodified** project under its
  name; build a fork and call it "based on SOLECTRUS" (with your own name).
- **You may not:** name your fork or product "SOLECTRUS"; offer "SOLECTRUS
  Hosting" or any paid service under the name; use the name or logo to look
  official, endorsed, or certified when it isn't; register the name, logo, or
  similar domains/handles as your own.

The sections below explain this in detail.

## Code vs. trademark

The **source code** of SOLECTRUS is free and open source, released under the
[AGPL-3.0](LICENSE). You may use, study, modify, and redistribute it under the
terms of that license.

The AGPL grants copyright permissions for the software. It does **not** grant you
permission to use the SOLECTRUS name or logo for your own product, fork,
distribution, or service.

## What you may do

You may use the SOLECTRUS name to refer truthfully to the SOLECTRUS project — for
example in documentation, compatibility statements ("compatible with SOLECTRUS"),
tutorials, reviews, or links. You may also redistribute the **unmodified** project
under the SOLECTRUS name.

These uses must be truthful and must not suggest that your work is official or
endorsed by Georg Ledermann or the SOLECTRUS project when it is not.

## Forks and modified versions

If you distribute a modified version, fork, or derivative, or run it as a service,
you must use a **different name and logo**. You may state factually that your work
is **"based on SOLECTRUS"**, as long as this does not imply official status,
affiliation, or endorsement.

The SOLECTRUS name identifies the genuine software as published by the project. If
you change the software — including disabling, bypassing, altering, or removing
built-in functionality — the result is no longer "SOLECTRUS". You must not use the
SOLECTRUS name or logo for such a modified version when you distribute it, publish
it, run it as a service, or otherwise make it publicly accessible.

This policy does not prevent you from keeping copyright notices, license notices,
attribution notices, or other legal notices that are required by the AGPL or are
otherwise appropriate.

## What you may not do

You may not use the SOLECTRUS name or logo in a way that suggests that your
project, product, service, fork, hosting offer, or distribution is officially
provided, endorsed, certified, or maintained by Georg Ledermann, unless you have
written permission. In particular, you may not:

- Use "SOLECTRUS" (or a confusingly similar name) as the name of your own product,
  company, fork, distribution, or hosted/commercial service.
- Modify the software and continue to present or label a publicly accessible
  instance as "SOLECTRUS".
- Brand a hosted or commercial offering as "SOLECTRUS" or "SOLECTRUS Hosting"
  (or similar) without written permission.
- Alter, recolor, or distort the logo and present it as official SOLECTRUS
  branding.
- Register the SOLECTRUS name, logo, or a confusingly similar name or logo as your
  own trademark.
- Register or use domains, package names, container image names, app names, or
  social media handles in a way that suggests official status, affiliation, or
  endorsement when none exists.
